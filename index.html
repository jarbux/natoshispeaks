Natoshi Speaks

A secret visit with the most wanted pseudonym in the world.

By JB
February 01, 2017

"I never said that." —Satoshi Nakamoto, Creator of Bitcoin

It's It's October 31st, 2017. A waterfall of emotions falls across me as I sit in front of the elusive Natoshi Sakamoto. I'm sweating bullets, which is inconceivable given the location. From what I can tell, we're in Russia. After 45 days of dead signal zones, nondescript intercontinental travel, and personal guides who communicate by writing on letters they quickly destroy afterwards I've made the summit. For weeks it felt like I was on a wild coin chase to track down the designer of currently the world's most groundbreaking disruption.

Bitcoin has outgrown economies, established new governmental protocols, threatened the very fabric of centralized institutions, and oppositely created the fabric for other decentralized processes. The currency and the Blockchain its built on has inspired the creation of new currencies, creating wealth from thin air. With Natoshi's wealth estimated at $12 billion and rapidly rising to becoming the first relatively publicized trillionaire it's no surprise that governments have ransacked homes and hacked computer systems searching for Sakamoto all to no avail.

Sakamoto has always remained elusive. Until now but it's blatantly clear you'll only find Natoshi if Natoshi wants you to.

The Invitation

I had been writing a blog for about two years. My goal was simple: explore the depths of the digital currency world and report back with the most promising applications. My blog wasn't exactly popular but it did gain some international traction and ultimately an invitation to interview Natoshi Sakamoto.

I was midway through completing an article about Jarbux, a new fiat-crypto hybrid that turns saving money into a game, connecting traditional savings habits with a new way to build wealth, cryptocurrency. I received a comment notification on my blog from a previous post.

It read, check mail.

Checking my mail was the single greatest thing I've done (outside of marrying my wife of course. I love you, baby). I opened up an unstamped and unaddressed envelope containing a letter and a Trac Phone. The abridged version of the letter: 'Natoshi Sakamoto would like to meet with you. Your work has been followed and you have been chosen to document the story.' That was followed up by a few minute instructions on the phone.

I opened it up: no service and no missed calls, but there were unsent text messages saved in the draft folder. What ensued was a list of instructions, directions, places to be and exact times at which to be there. Skepticism racked my mind but there was one aspect of it all that made me feel that it could be real. The last message I opened was a web address and a string of letters and numbers.

It was access to a private Bitcoin key. Total value: $1.3 million. This was real.

The Background

In 2008 a Whitepaper was released on a new peer-to-peer electronic cash system. While not the first attempt at a digital currency it has become the most criticized, loved, famed, and feared. The introduction's first sentence speaks volumes of Sakamoto's intentions that I would come to understand more deeply as we talked. It read "commerce on the Internet has come to rely almost exclusively on financial institutions serving as trusted third parties to process electronic payments." The one word that sounds out like an alarm to the masses - trust.

It is this trust or rather lack of trust that predicated the evolution of Bitcoin as a concept into Bitcoin as a collective creation. Distrust in centralized organizations. Distrust in mismatched financial priorities. Distrust in what Sakamoto calls the illusion of transparency, solidarity, and longevity. Sakamoto believes financial systems have devolved over time because true evolution is through a collective alignment of principles and processes as opposed to an isolation and fear based protection of them.

In 2008 the Bitcoin Whitepaper was released, subsequently in 2010 following initial mining its value hovered below $1. It's 2018 and at one point Bitcoin rose above $19,000 but Sakamoto would be quick to tell you that if you're following the value in isolation you are missing the point. Value is simply a proxy for its underlying architecture to create what Natoshi iterates as the evolution of trust and exchange. Trust is fleeting, wavering, blind faith. It's evolution, however, is proof oriented, without a middleman, it' is cryptographic.

Sakamoto likens historical financial institution trust to religion, placing faith in an ambiguous figure that's been centralized and communicated through institutions like the church or the mosque. The evolution of that, despite religious affiliation is a more direct link to the source, a low friction exchange that can be seen and accessed by the whole yet still protected. Think of a collective consciousness that is out there for everyone to access but your individual prayers or transactions have a protected direct link. Esoteric, yes. Paradoxical in nature, absolutely but it's exactly what Sakamoto achieved, the evolution of trust and exchange.

The Preparation

This was a rare occasion and I wasn't about to screw it up. Imagine being presented with the opportunity to meet Natoshi Sakamoto. The exchange that I would have couldn't be anything less than penetrating. In conjunction with that mindset I began to frame questions that would get to the core of Sakamoto outside of what the world has come to know or think they know.

I read scores of articles, reports of people claiming to be Sakamoto, and documentation on Bitcoin itself. If it had something to do with him, her, them, or even it I read it. I found information ranging from detailing the creation of Bitcoin as a covert Artificial Intelligence operation to it being a governmental operation in preparation for an impending economic Y2K or E2K as the skeptics would have you call it.

The research led me to one place that no one had yet discovered. Assumptions made, advocated, and perpetuated yet none scratched the surface of exactly who Sakamoto is. Finding out changed my world forever. Some assumptions were validated while others were simply shrugged off and disregarded as folklore. I gained a personal look into Sakamoto's existence and that word is used intentionally as Sakamoto would tell you, it isn't just about Sakamoto.

"Look beyond the blind faith you've placed in systems and institutions and what they represent. Much like music is found in the silence between the notes, the real nature of myself, Bitcoin, and this framework is in what is yet to be seen and found beyond what people think they are capable of seeing." - Natoshi Sakamoto

When you don't think about it, it's not thatcryptic.

The Interview

Shortened for brevity, the full audio interview can be found here

The air is crisp. We're in a nondescript cottage with a kettle on the stove boiling water for tea. The cottage is simple and comfortable, it fits in. There are no armed guards, no expensive cars - just a person who appears to be living modestly, but not questionably modestly. The only real inkling of a connection to the decentralized world is a framed picture on the wall. Tough to make out at first, Sakamoto gestures for me to get up from the table and take a look.

It's a few handwritten pages, the original blueprint for what ultimately became Bitcoin's whitepaper. My mind immediately races to how much it could possibly be worth. Then I look around and realize that the question of worth as I have come to know and value is inconsequential and beneath Sakamoto. I sit back down the tea begins to whistle.

JB: Is it just you?

Natoshi: Never was. Never will be.

How many people are involved?

Natoshi: People, governmental institutions, NGO's, systems - the number grows every day.

JB: People find out who you are. Governments set their eyes on finding you. What do you do?

The money Sakamoto has helped bring into this world is digital, adapting itself to different markets and expectations. What goes unacknowledged by the majority of people is that the color their money is printed on despite origin is always red. Blood is spilled everyday by our leaders, politicians, and executives. We give credence to the institutions that tell us the worth of the money that we work to create, but that money is used to privately fund wars, create educational systems that fit people into pre-designed boxes, and build products on tired backs when brains should be used. We unknowingly perpetuate that system.

What happens if you get caught?

Caught for what? Tyranny, destabilizing economies, creating a medium of exchange? You, much like the majority of the world exploring this space make a very ill rooted assumption - that this is about money. People look towards my relative fiat wealth and base their assumptions on that, missing the underlying fundamentals of its creation. Right now people are fishing for me with a net when I'm not in the river.

Where are you then?

I live in the black market.

What do you think about Bitcoin being used for black market exchanges?

Black is beautiful. Objectively, every market can be black depending on its utility; it's a different world that society is not yet ready to accept. While many have used these markets poorly, others have used them to transport support and aid to those in need. I live in that market. I was born in it, molded by it. The world has yet to even adopt it. These shadows betray the world because the world betrays them.

What do you think about when you're by yourself?

My family, their future, the implications of emerging cryptocurrencies and decentralized technologies.

Other crypto currencies?

There have been digital currencies before. There will be digital currencies long after Sakamoto is gone. I think many are great and the nature of improving on Bitcoin and the original mission has come as a pleasant surprise. There were dynamics at play that were considered but for the sake of creating something together as a collective, we published.

Do you have any favorite currencies?

Jarbux. Gamification, behavioral science, user psychology - I'll be honest - these terms are all commoditized and mean nothing to me. What I find unique about the Jarbux platform is that its true impact is concealed under the veil of a mobile gaming app. People believe they are just having fun saving money and building wealth for the future. Yet all along, the platform and its crypto token will help the mass market adopt new savings behaviors and leverage cryptocurrency as wealth builder without challenging their day-to-day habits. Things don't have to be all-or-none a world made up solely of crypto. It's all about balancing the realities of the world we've come to know with the incentive to explore the world that is on the horizon.

ICOs launch, wallets are compromised, people are taken advantage of. What are your thoughts?

Don't run when you should be walking. Stay in your lane until you're ready to put your blinkers on. There are plenty of tools, MyEtherWallet, Ledger Wallets, etc. that help encrypt and protect this global transition.

How are you protecting yourself?

I'm more connected to the strength of global economies than is publicly represented. The very institutions that the media says will get disrupted are the ones where Sakamoto has a seat at the table.

What is your biggest regret in life?

Well, this one time at band camp.

I see you're hip to pop culture. Here's one. What do you think of Tinder?

I coded a private version between my wife and I where we'd only match with ourselves. The kicker was that we each created 10 different personality profiles so whoever matched we'd have to go on a date as those personalities.

But really, what's your biggest regret in life?

I plan my regrets. My biggest regrets comes in 5 years where I publicize Sakamoto to the world. It's necessary but will bring turmoil.

What happens in 5 years?

The Transition.

Are you putting your family at risk?

The biggest risk in life is not to take one.

Why did you choose me to cover this?

I think you know the answer to that. Full answer in exclusive audio interview here.

If I ask you to define yourself as a person, to pretend you are not Sakamoto, instead you are the person who knows Sakamoto better than anybody else in the world, how would you define Natoshi?

I wouldn't define Sakamoto. Definitions of people crystallize the ether that they're made up of. Digital currency is not unlike the fabric of our existence - Who truly demands your accountability? Defines your value? Tracks it over time, validates it, verifies it? The society around you that you let deem your worth, they are the Blockchain constantly reinforcing the value you bring to the world. Sakamoto isn't this, Sakamoto does not exist within the frame of its own creation, it uses its creation to evolve itself.
